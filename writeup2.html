Consider the function g shown in Figure 4.5.9.

Let `G` be an antiderivative of `g`. Suppose that `G` is continuous on `[-6,6]`, `G(-6)=-3`, and that the greatest value `G` ever achieves is `6`.

Line segments in `g` will be labeled `l_1` to `l_4` ordered from left to right.

Similarly, `G` will be split into segements from `L_1` to `L_4`.


`G(-6) = -3`


`l_1` implies `L_1` is an exponential curve. The area under `l_1` is `6 + 6/2`, so `G` will rise `9` units over `[-6, -3]`.

`G(-3) = G(-6) + 9 = 6`


`l_2` implies `L_2` is linear. The area under `l_2` is `-12`, so `G` will rise `-12` units over `[-3, 1]`.

`G(1) = G(-3) - 12 = - 6`


`L_3` is similar to `L_1`

`G(4) = G(1) + 9 = 3`


`l_4` implies `L_4` is linear. The area under `l_4` is `-6`, so `G` will rise `-6` units over `[4, 6]`.

`G(6) = G(4) - 6 = -3`

5.8.1.6

This is the quotient rule:

`d/dx(f(x)/g(x)) = (d/dx(f(x)) * g(x) - f(x) * d/dx(g(x)))/(g(x)^2)`

Let us use the quotient rule to evaluate the following expression:

`d/dx(x^6/x^2)`

`= (d/dx(x^6) * x^2 - x^6 * d/dx(x^2))/(x^4)`

`= (6x^5 * x^2 - x^6 * 2x)/(x^4)`

`= (6x^7 - 2x^7)/(x^4)`

`= 6x^3 - 2x^3`

`= 4x^3`

Now, to check our work, we will first simplify the expression, then evaluate without using the quotient rule.

`d/dx(x^6/x^2)`

`= d/dx(x^4)`

`= 4x^3`

Both methods yield the same result.

6.1.1.14

Let `f(t) = te^t - e^(t^2) + 3`.

There is an antiderivative of `f` that passes through the point `(0,7)`. Find the equation of the tangent line to this antiderivative at `0`.

We don't need to know the antiderivative to answer this. All we need is a point and a slope. The point is `(0,7)`. Slope of the antiderivative is given by the original function `f`.

`x * f(0) + 7`

`= x * (0e^0 - e^(0^2) + 3) + 7`

`= x * (0 - 1 + 3) + 7`

`= 2x + 7`

`y = 2x + 7` describes a tangent line to `F` that passes through `(0,7)` where `F` is an antiderivative of `f`
